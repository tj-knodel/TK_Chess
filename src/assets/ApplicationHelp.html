Help With Using the Application

Here we have help on how to use the application itself, as well as some background and rules to learn chess

Using the Application

  • Upon opening the application, You will be able to select which game-mode you would like to play.
  • If you ever wish to restart a game, you can select "Game" -> "Restart Game"

How To Enter A Move?

  • Either select a piece and select where you would like to move it to.
  • Or, using the text input, type in your move and press enter to submit the move.

Customizing the Application

You can change the application appearance in many different ways:

  • Resize the GUI window
  • You can toggle whether the board coordinates are shown or not.
    • This can be done under "Board Appearance" -> "Toggle Coordinates"
  • You can toggle whether the possible moves are shown or not.
    • This can be done under "Board Appearance" -> "Toggle Possible Moves"
  • You can toggle whether a previous move highlight is shown or not.
    • This can be done under "Board Appearance" -> "Toggle Previous Moves"
  • The board its can have its tiles and piece icons changed
    • This can be done by selecting "Board Appearance" -> "Set Board Appearance". A popup will show where you can select a pre-included appearance or select a custom appearance.
  • You can flip the board (switch between which color is on the bottom).
    • This can be done by selecting "Board Appearance" -> "Flip Board"

Background

Chess is a very old game. We have records of its existence from the 7th century. Since them, it has gone through many changes, such as the elephant piece being changed to the bishop, and some rules that speed up and make the game quite interesting. The rules we know of today emerged in Europe around the 15th century, with the rules being standardized with the rise of organized chess tournaments at the end of the 19th century. Modern chess has 5 different pieces: pawns, rooks, knights, queens, and the all important kings, and has rules for castling, en passant, and pawn double move, which will be explained more fully later in this document.

Rules of the Game

You may have heard of all sorts of Gambits, Mates, Openings, and such, but those are strategies and you can learn about those later on. The rules of the game are actually fairly simple. The goal of the game is to put the opposing colours King in checkmate. I will explain checkmate in a little bit.

Each piece moves in a particular way, but there are some general rules. A piece can't move in such away that it puts your own king in check, which we will get to later when we talk about checkmate. A piece can't capture its own colours piece. Capturing is when you move a piece onto the opposing colours piece, removing it from the board. Now, onto the movement. First I will start with the simple ones.

The rook, the one that looks like a little castle, can move as far as possible in either a horizontal or vertical direction. It has a special move with the king where if there are no spaces between the king and the rook and neither of them have moved, you can essentially swap places. This is a very useful move to protect your precious king. You can see an example of this in the tutorial section.

The bishop moves diagonally across the board as far as it can go. Very simple.

The knight has the unique ability to "jump" across pieces. It moves in an L shape, two spaces away and one space to the side.

The queen has the movement of both the bishop and the rook, thus making it a very important piece.

The pawn is an odd one. For their first move, they can move up to two spaces. After that, they can only move one space. They can only capture a piece that is diagonal to them, oddly enough. They also have a unique ability called "en passant" which is difficult to explain, but we have a section for it in the tutorial section.

The king can move one space in any direction. Very simple, very boring. It can't move into any space where another piece can potentially capture it.

When yours or the opponents king is attacked by a piece but is able to get into safety somehow (on a square that is not being attacked), this is called check. Then for checkmate, this means that your colour's pieces attacks the opponents King in such away that it cannot move to a space where it isn't attacked. Naturally, you want to avoid your King being checkmated as well. You can see an example of checkmate in the tutorial section.

Finally, there are some unique situations where the game can end in a draw. If you and your opponent only have a king left on the board, that is stalemate and the game is a draw. If you get into a situation where you or your opponent is in check but cannot make a legal move, that is stalemate. And the game can also end in a draw if no piece has been captured and no pawn has moved in the last 50 moves. You may not have to worry about this, but best to keep in mind.

And those are all the rules to chess. The best way to learn strategy is just to start playing! Enjoy!
